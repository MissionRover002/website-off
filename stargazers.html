Stargazers


Mystery is hidden more in far off places than the things close to you. This is what unites a group of young minds at Stargazers to study the Universe, find clues, and understand the universe better. At team Stargazers we cover all things Space. From fun events like watching the eclipse, to quizzes that get your brain thinking. We have done events and case studies that have been showcased in events.

Each individual member of Stargazers chose a topic of their interest from the vast field of cosmology and astronomy and did case studies on them. The case studies were done on topics ranging from Population 3 Stars, Magnetic Monopoles, Light in Space to the James Webb Telescope. In the future we plan to do a series of these case studies in the order of the formation of the universe. Starting from the Big Bang all the way to the present.

Case Studies

  • Magnetic Monopoles in Neutron Stars by Ibrahim Chikani.

Scientists have discovered magnetic monopoles in neutron stars. Magnetic monopoles came into the picture when the symmetry "broke" while trying to unify electromagnetism and weak interaction. After the Big Bang, the universe expanded at a rapid rate which diluted the already scarce population of monopoles. The core of the Neutron star is neutron-rich. Magnetic monopoles require extremely high temperatures of around 1011K, such extreme temperatures are difficult to replicate on any collider on Earth or any other heavenly body. Magnetic monopoles thrive in conditions of high temperatures, high pressure, and very high magnetic fields. These conditions are exhibited again in newly formed neutron stars which are milli-seconds young. This makes magnetars suitable candidates to gather data from to collect slight pieces of evidence if available, to help detect signatures of magnetic monopoles. Isolated neutron stars emit gravitational waves in various ways. Milli-magnetic monopoles, although yet to be observed in nature, are very commonly seen in high-energy physics equations and Unified theory equations. These magnetic monopoles are used for the prediction of model frameworks in most of the quantum field theories. Our focus is mainly on the deformations caused in the star due to the magnetic fields produced inside the core of the star.

  • Neutrino Release from a Black Hole by M. Ravi Shankar Somanadha.

A black hole is a tremendous amount of matter and energy packed into a tiny space of area comparatively. As such, it also absorbs and releases energy and matter into space. One such matter released is a Neutrino, a microscopic particle unseen by the naked eye. It is perceived to be released by the black holes as the release neutrino bursts and the energy release timings nearly coincide with each other. As such, three observatories observed and timed these energy bursts together. They are the Chandra X-ray Observatory, the Swift gamma-ray mission, and the Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescope Array. All three observatories paired up with IceCube Neutrino Observatory, wherein The scientists analyzed the Data gathered by the observatories. These observatories conducted several tests and observations where they sent around 37 X-Ray Bursts, out of which two events numbered #25 and #33 were recorded as the closest events occurring after the energy bursts, counting about 2:38:29 hours after the X-ray flare. Pythia8 was used to simulate the production of neutrino and photons from the inelastic scattering of a cosmic ray proton with the proton at rest. The timings of IC NU events from Sagittarius A* are sometimes correlated with the observed photon flaring in X-rays at the G.C. In particular, the timing and approximate positional coincidences of I.C. #25 and Chandra #14392 as an an indicator that Sagittarius A* is considered to be the source of I.C.

  • Stellar Population Stars by Manasa Sruthi Viswanadha.

Originally conceived by Jan Oort in 1926, Walter Baade categorized groups of stars within the Milky Way into Stellar Populations in 1944. The case study discusses Stellar Population Stars, their classifications and the available data from previous research papers and studies. A group of stars within the galaxy that resemble each other in spatial distribution, chemical composition, or age. Stellar populations exhibit a continuum of attributes rather than discrete ones, which represent variations in star formation through time. The two main divisions were :- Population 1(Pop.1) and Population 2(Pop.2). A newer hypothetical division was added in 1978 called, Population 3(Pop.3) These three population classes usefully divided stars by their chemical composition or metallicity. Each population group shows the trend where decreasing metal content indicates increasing age of stars. The most recent stars are the Pop.1 stars with high metallicity; The old stars are Pop.2 stars with low metallicity. And, the first stars that were formed right after the Big Bang are the oldest ones called the Pop.3 stars. Most of the Population 1 Stars orbit the galactic center in regular slow-moving elliptical orbits. Because planets, especially, are expected to develop via the accretion of metals, it was previously hypothesized that population 1 stars, which are more metallic than the other two populations, are more likely to have planetary systems. The stars classified as Population 2 or metal-poor have a dearth of elements heavier than helium. These were formed earlier during the history of the universe. Numerous population 2 stars are also present in Globular Clusters or GCs. Two different kinds of stars have been referred to as Pop.3 stars :- Those that originate from pristine gas left over from cosmic nucleosynthesis and those that are thought to be the source for dark matter in galactic halos. Either type could be pre-galactic or formed during the most initial stages of galaxy formation. Pop.3 stars might not exist anymore, but if they do, they deserve a unique name as they have intriguing cosmological composition. Scientists are actively researching these. This case study further provides and discusses all three Stellar Population Stars.
